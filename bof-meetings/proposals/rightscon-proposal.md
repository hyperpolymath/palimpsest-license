# RightsCon Session Proposal: Palimpsest License Framework

## Session Title
**From Extraction to Consent: Protecting Marginalised Creators' Rights in the AI Training Era**

## Session Format
**Interactive Discussion** (90 minutes)

## Abstract
AI systems built on massive web scraping extract creative labour from the most vulnerable: diaspora storytellers, trauma survivors, indigenous knowledge keepers, protest artists, and marginalised communities whose narratives fuel billion-dollar AI models without consent or compensation. This session examines the Palimpsest License frameworkâ€”a rights-based approach to creative attribution designed to protect emotional lineage, cultural context, and symbolic significance. We centre voices from marginalised creator communities to develop advocacy strategies, technical safeguards, and governance mechanisms that shift power from extractive AI corporations to those whose stories and struggles are being commodified.

## Problem Statement

### The Human Rights Crisis
**AI training as cultural extraction:**
1. **Diaspora narratives** are scraped from personal blogs, translated without consent, stripped of cultural context
2. **Trauma testimony** from survivors becomes training data for chatbots that trivialise suffering
3. **Protest art** and resistance imagery train surveillance systems used against activists
4. **Indigenous knowledge** is extracted, decontextualised, and sold back without attribution
5. **Marginalised language communities** lose control over linguistic and cultural heritage

### Power Asymmetries
- **Global North corporations** extract value from Global South creators
- **Tech platforms** claim ownership over community-generated content
- **AI systems** perpetuate bias whilst erasing authorship of marginalised voices
- **Legal systems** favour capital over creators, especially those without resources for litigation
- **Existing licenses** (Creative Commons, etc.) designed for different era, fail to address AI-specific harms

### Rights at Stake
- **Cultural rights** (UDHR Article 27, ICESCR Article 15)
- **Freedom of expression** (ICCPR Article 19)
- **Right to privacy and dignity** (UDHR Articles 12, 22)
- **Indigenous peoples' rights** (UNDRIP Articles 11, 31)
- **Economic justice and fair compensation** (ICESCR Article 7)
- **Protection from exploitation** (ICCPR Article 8)

### Why RightsCon?
This is fundamentally a **human rights issue** requiring:
- Centering marginalised voices in technical design
- Challenging corporate power and extractive systems
- Building solidarity across creator communities
- Developing advocacy strategies for systemic change
- Connecting digital rights to economic and cultural justice

## Rights-Based Framework

### Human Rights Foundations
**Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)**
- **Article 27(1)**: Right to participate in cultural life
- **Article 27(2)**: Right to protection of moral and material interests in creative work

**International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR)**
- **Article 15**: Right to benefit from protection of moral and material interests in creative work

**UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP)**
- **Article 11**: Right to maintain, protect and develop cultural heritage
- **Article 31**: Right to control cultural heritage, traditional knowledge and cultural expressions

### Justice Principles
- **Epistemic justice**: Recognition of marginalised ways of knowing
- **Distributive justice**: Fair compensation and resource allocation
- **Procedural justice**: Meaningful participation in decision-making
- **Restorative justice**: Repair for harms and extraction
- **Transformative justice**: Systemic change to prevent future harm

### Intersectional Analysis
- **Race and colonialism**: AI extraction as digital colonialism
- **Class and labour**: Creative work as undervalued labour
- **Gender and patriarchy**: Gendered dimensions of cultural extraction
- **Disability justice**: Ableist design of attribution systems
- **Migration and borders**: Citizenship status and legal protections

## Advocacy Demands

### For AI Companies
1. **Obtain explicit consent** before using creative work in training data
2. **Compensate creators** fairly for use of their work
3. **Preserve attribution and context** in training datasets and outputs
4. **Provide transparency** about training data sources
5. **Enable meaningful opt-out** mechanisms that are actually honoured
6. **Establish community governance** with creator representation
7. **Remediate harms** to communities already affected by extraction

### For Platforms
1. **Implement consent mechanisms** for AI training on user content
2. **Preserve metadata** through caching and content transformation
3. **Enforce license terms** and provide recourse for violations
4. **Support collective action** by creator communities
5. **Default to privacy and protection** rather than extraction

### For Regulators and Policymakers
1. **Recognise emotional and cultural rights** beyond narrow copyright
2. **Centre marginalised communities** in AI regulation
3. **Provide legal aid and resources** for enforcement
4. **Address power asymmetries** through structural intervention
5. **Align with international human rights law**
