# Palimpsest License - Legal Strategy Document v0.4

## Executive Summary

This document outlines the legal strategy for the Palimpsest License, identifying critical legal questions, jurisdictional challenges, enforcement mechanisms, and international harmonisation requirements. It serves as a roadmap for pro bono legal engagement and policy advocacy.

**Primary Goal:** Ensure Palimpsest License is legally valid, enforceable, and compliant across key jurisdictions whilst achieving its protective purpose for creators.

**Primary Jurisdiction:** Netherlands (Dutch law governs)
**Secondary Jurisdictions:** United Kingdom (England & Wales, Scotland), European Union, United States
**Global Applicability:** International enforceability and harmonisation

## Critical Legal Questions

### A. Dutch Law Compliance (PRIMARY - HIGHEST PRIORITY)

#### 1. Auteurswet (Copyright Act) Alignment

**Question 1.1: Moral Rights (Article 25 Auteurswet)**
- **Issue:** Does Palimpsest's "emotional lineage" concept lawfully extend Article 25's paternity (right to attribution) and integrity (right to object to derogatory treatment) rights?
- **Why it matters:** Moral rights are inalienable under Dutch law; we must ensure we're not claiming rights beyond legal scope
- **Who to ask:** IViR (Prof. Hugenholtz), Dutch copyright law specialists, De Brauw Blackstone Westbroek
- **Priority:** üî¥ Critical

**Question 1.2: Text and Data Mining Exception (Articles 45c-45h Auteurswet - DSM Directive transposition)**
- **Issue:** Does Palimpsest's requirement for explicit consent for "non-interpretive AI" comply with Article 45d's opt-out mechanism?
- **Sub-questions:**
  - Can we require opt-in where law provides opt-out?
  - What constitutes "machine-readable" opt-out under Dutch implementation?
  - Does "commercial TDM" (Article 45d) include AI training for generative models?
- **Why it matters:** If our consent requirement conflicts with statutory TDM exception, license may be unenforceable
- **Who to ask:** IViR (Prof. Quintais - DSM Directive expert), Dutch Copyright Association, NautaDutilh
- **Priority:** üî¥ Critical

**Question 1.3: Economic Rights - Derivative Works (Articles 1, 12-13 Auteurswet)**
- **Issue:** Can we legally distinguish between "human-created derivatives" (permitted with attribution) and "AI-generated derivatives" (requiring explicit consent)?
- **Sub-questions:**
  - Does Dutch law recognise this distinction?
  - Is "AI-generated" a legally meaningful category?
  - Can licensing terms restrict derivative works based on creation method?
- **Why it matters:** Core of Palimpsest's AI protection mechanism
- **Who to ask:** IViR, Freshfields Amsterdam, Stibbe
- **Priority:** üî¥ Critical

**Question 1.4: Waiver and Licensing of Rights**
- **Issue:** Can moral rights be waived or licensed in the manner Palimpsest proposes under Dutch law?
- **Dutch law generally:** Moral rights are inalienable but can be contractually managed
- **Palimpsest approach:** We don't waive moral rights; we extend them
- **Who to ask:** IViR, Dutch Civil Code specialists
- **Priority:** üü† Very High

#### 2. Contract Law Validity (Burgerlijk Wetboek - Dutch Civil Code)

**Question 2.1: Contractual Enforceability**
- **Issue:** Are Palimpsest's licensing terms valid contracts under Books 3 and 6 of the Dutch Civil Code?
- **Sub-questions:**
  - Offer and acceptance in license context?
  - Consideration (if required)?
  - Third-party beneficiary rights (for enforcement)?
- **Who to ask:** Dutch contract law specialists, law firms
- **Priority:** üü† Very High

**Question 2.2: Standard Form Contracts (Algemene Voorwaarden)**
- **Issue:** Does Palimpsest risk classification as unfair standard terms (oneerlijke bedingen)?
- **Relevant law:** Book 6, Title 5, Section 3 (Articles 233-247 BW)
- **Risk:** Some clauses might be invalidated if deemed disproportionately burdensome
- **Who to ask:** Dutch consumer/contract law experts
- **Priority:** üü† Very High

**Question 2.3: International Contracts - Choice of Law**
- **Issue:** Can we effectively stipulate Dutch law governs internationally?
- **Relevant:** Rome I Regulation (EU), Hague Principles (international)
- **Who to ask:** International contract law specialists, firms with cross-border practice
- **Priority:** üü† Very High

#### 3. EU Law Compliance

**Question 3.1: DSM Directive (2019/790) - Full Compliance**
- **Issue:** Beyond TDM, how do other DSM Directive provisions interact with Palimpsest?
- **Relevant Articles:**
  - Article 17: Platform liability for copyright content
  - Article 15: Press publishers' rights
  - Articles 3-6: Cultural heritage exceptions
- **Who to ask:** IViR, Max Planck Institute, EU copyright scholars
- **Priority:** üü† Very High

**Question 3.2: AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) Alignment**
- **Issue:** When AI Act applies (2025-2026), how do transparency requirements interact with Palimpsest?
- **Relevant:** Article 53 (transparency for generative AI)
- **Opportunity:** Palimpsest's metadata requirements could complement AI Act obligations
- **Who to ask:** TILT (Tilburg), AI law specialists, Bits of Freedom
- **Priority:** üü° High (timing: pre-2026)

**Question 3.3: Data Protection (GDPR) - Metadata as Personal Data**

- **Opportunity:** If yes, GDPR's enforcement mechanisms could supplement copyright enforcement

**Question 5.2: Moral Rights (17 USC ¬ß 106A - VARA)**
- **Issue:** US moral rights extremely limited (visual arts only, not literature/music)
- **Question:** Can Palimpsest's moral rights protections be enforced in US as contract terms?
- **Who to ask:** US copyright scholars, EFF
- **Priority:** üü° High

**Question 5.3: International Enforceability - Choice of Law**
- **Issue:** Will US courts recognise Dutch law choice-of-law clause?
- **Relevant:** Conflicts of law principles vary by state
- **Who to ask:** International IP litigators, academic conflicts scholars

## Enforcement Mechanisms

### A. Copyright Infringement Claims

**Mechanism:** Traditional copyright litigation for unauthorised use

**Applicable When:**
- User creates derivative without complying with Palimpsest terms
- AI company uses work for training without consent
- Platform strips metadata in violation of preservation clause

**Advantages:**
- ‚úÖ Established legal framework
- ‚úÖ Statutory damages available (some jurisdictions)
- ‚úÖ Injunctive relief possible
- ‚úÖ Precedent exists for copyright enforcement

**Challenges:**
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Expensive (litigation costs)
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Slow (years for resolution)
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Uncertain outcome (fair use, TDM exceptions)
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Requires proving actual infringement

**Strategic Considerations:**
- Choose test cases carefully (strong facts, sympathetic plaintiff)
- Seek pro bono litigation support (SFLC model, PIIPA, EFF)
- Consider alternative dispute resolution first

---

### B. Contract Breach Claims

**Mechanism:** Sue for breach of license agreement (contract law)

**Applicable When:**
- Licensee violates terms (e.g., uses for AI training without consent)
- Platform fails to preserve metadata as contractually required
- Third party induces breach of contract

**Advantages:**
- ‚úÖ May avoid copyright defences (fair use applies to copyright, not contracts)
- ‚úÖ Liquidated damages clauses possible
- ‚úÖ Potentially faster than copyright litigation
- ‚úÖ Contractual remedies (specific performance, etc.)

**Challenges:**
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Privity of contract issues (who can sue whom?)
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Enforcing against third parties difficult
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Contract law varies significantly by jurisdiction
- ‚ö†Ô∏è May be seen as overriding copyright policy (courts reluctant)

**Strategic Considerations:**
- Draft clear contract formation language (acceptance, click-through, etc.)
- Consider third-party beneficiary clauses (allow creators to enforce)
- Research contracts-override-copyright precedents by jurisdiction

---

### C. Platform Accountability

**Mechanism:** Require platforms to enforce license terms via notice-and-takedown or similar

**Applicable When:**
- Platform hosts Palimpsest-licensed content
- Users violate license terms on platform
- Platform strips metadata

**Legal Basis:**
- EU: DSA Article 17 (DSM Directive), DSA notice mechanisms
- US: DMCA ¬ß 512 (though limited to copyright)
- UK: Similar regimes

**Advantages:**
- ‚úÖ Leverages existing platform compliance systems
- ‚úÖ Faster than litigation (notice ‚Üí takedown)
- ‚úÖ Platforms have resources to enforce
- ‚úÖ Scales better than individual litigation

**Challenges:**
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Platforms may not recognise custom licenses (only standard ones)
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Safe harbour protections limit platform liability
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Over-enforcement risk (false positives, censorship)
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Requires machine-readable licensing

**Strategic Considerations:**
- Engage platforms early (GitHub, Medium, Substack, etc.)
- Develop machine-readable metadata standards (JSON-LD, RDF)
- Partner with Creative Commons on platform integration
- Advocate for platform policy changes to recognise Palimpsest

### D. Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)

**Mechanism:** Mediation, arbitration, or expert determination

**Applicable When:**
- Parties willing to resolve outside courts
- Faster, cheaper resolution needed
- International disputes (avoid jurisdictional complexity)

**Options:**
- **WIPO Arbitration and Mediation Center:** Specialises in IP disputes

- **Online Dispute Resolution (ODR):** For platform-based disputes

**Advantages:**
- ‚úÖ Faster than litigation
- ‚úÖ Less expensive
- ‚úÖ Confidential (may be preferred)
- ‚úÖ Parties choose arbitrator/mediator (expertise)
- ‚úÖ Enforceable internationally (New York Convention)

**Challenges:**
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Requires both parties' agreement
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Limited precedent-setting value
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Arbitration costs still significant
- ‚ö†Ô∏è Limited appeal rights

**Strategic Considerations:**
- Include ADR clause in license (mediation first, then arbitration)
- Specify WIPO or ICC as provider (credibility, IP expertise)

## International Harmonisation

### Challenge: Multi-Jurisdictional Licensing

**Problem:** Single license must work across different legal systems with varying:
- Copyright doctrines (moral rights strong in EU, weak in US)
- Exceptions (fair use in US, fair dealing in UK, EU exceptions)
- Contract law (enforceability, formation, remedies)
- Platform regulation

**Approaches:**

#### Option 1: Single Universal License with Choice of Law
- **Model:** Current Palimpsest approach - Dutch law governs, applies internationally
- **Precedent:** Creative Commons 3.0 unported
- **Advantages:** Simplicity, consistency
- **Challenges:** Foreign courts may not apply Dutch law; conflicts with local mandatory law

#### Option 2: Jurisdiction-Specific Versions (Creative Commons 4.0 Model)
- **Model:** Separate licenses for each jurisdiction (NL, UK, US, etc.)
- **Precedent:** Creative Commons jurisdiction ports (now deprecated in CC 4.0)
- **Advantages:** Tailored to local law, maximises enforceability
- **Challenges:** Complexity, inconsistency, maintenance burden

#### Option 3: Hybrid - Core Universal + Jurisdictional Modules
- **Model:** Universal core principles + jurisdiction-specific implementation guidance
- **Example:** Universal: "AI training requires consent"; US module: "without limiting fair use rights"
- **Advantages:** Balance of consistency and local adaptation
- **Challenges:** Complexity, requires multi-jurisdictional legal expertise

**Recommended Approach:**
- **Phase 1 (current):** Universal license with Dutch law choice (v0.4)
- **Phase 2 (post-legal review):** Add interpretive guidance for key jurisdictions
- **Phase 3 (if needed):** Develop full jurisdiction-specific variants

---

### Conflict of Laws Principles

**Key Questions:**

**1. Which law governs?**
- **Choice of law clause:** License states Dutch law (Palimpsest v0.4)
- **Enforceability:** Depends on jurisdiction's conflicts principles
  - **EU:** Rome I Regulation (parties can choose law for contracts)
  - **US:** Varies by state; generally respect choice of law if reasonable connection
  - **UK:** Similar to EU (Rome I incorporated into UK law pre-Brexit)

**2. Will foreign courts apply Dutch law?**
- **Generally yes if:**
  - Choice of law clause is clear
  - Dutch law doesn't violate foreign public policy
  - Reasonable connection to Netherlands (e.g., licensor based there, or explicit choice)
- **May not if:**
  - Conflicts with mandatory local law (e.g., moral rights inalienability)
  - Seen as evading consumer protection laws
  - No genuine connection to Netherlands

**Strategic Considerations:**
- Include explicit choice of law clause (already done)
- Establish "connection" to Netherlands (e.g., license drafted by Dutch entity, registered there)
- Consider choice of forum clause (Dutch courts have jurisdiction)
- Prepare for foreign courts applying local law despite clause

### Scenario 1: AI Company Trains on Palimpsest-Licensed Work Without Consent

**Facts:**
- Creator licenses novel under Palimpsest v0.4 (explicit "no AI training" without consent)
- AI company scrapes novel from public website for training generative AI
- AI company did not seek consent
- AI company's model later generates text similar to novel's style/themes

**Legal Questions:**
- Is AI training a "use" requiring copyright permission?
- Does TDM exception apply, or does Palimpsest opt-out override it?
- Can creator prove AI training occurred (discovery issues)?
- Is generated output a derivative work?

**Jurisdictions:**
- **Best:** Netherlands (Dutch law governs, strong moral rights)
- **Possible:** UK, EU member states (DSM Directive), US (fair use battle)

**Enforcement Strategy:**
1. Cease-and-desist letter citing license violation
2. If ignored: Copyright infringement + breach of contract claim
3. Seek injunction (stop training, delete model if possible)
4. Seek damages (statutory if available, or actual harm)
5. Public campaign highlighting AI exploitation

**Pro Bono Support:**

### Scenario 2: Platform Strips Metadata from Palimpsest-Licensed Work

**Facts:**
- Creator uploads short story to blogging platform with Palimpsest license
- Platform's CMS (content management system) strips metadata (license info, attribution, emotional context)
- Work is then shared/reused without license information
- Creator discovers violation, notifies platform
- Platform refuses to restore metadata or change CMS

**Legal Questions:**
- Can platform be held liable for metadata stripping?
- Is metadata "Copyright Management Information" under DMCA ¬ß 1202 or DSM Article 17?
- Does platform have duty to preserve license metadata?
- Is this GDPR violation if metadata includes personal data?

**Jurisdictions:**
- **Best:** Netherlands (if platform has EU presence - DSA applies)
- **Possible:** US (DMCA ¬ß 1202), UK (similar to EU)

**Enforcement Strategy:**
1. Formal notice to platform citing DSA, DMCA ¬ß 1202, license breach
2. If ignored: Regulatory complaint (DSA enforcement, DPA if GDPR)
3. Public campaign (social media, tech press)
4. Class action if multiple creators affected
5. Litigation if high-stakes (major platform)

**Pro Bono Support:**
- Digital rights organisations (EDRi, EFF, Bits of Freedom)
- Platform accountability specialists
- Data protection lawyers (GDPR angle)

**Policy Impact:**
- Establishes platform duty to preserve licensing metadata
- Informs DSA enforcement
- Creates precedent for custom license recognition

### Scenario 3: User Creates AI-Generated Derivative, Claims Fair Use/TDM

**Facts:**
- User trains local AI model on Palimpsest-licensed poetry collection
- User generates new poems in style of original using AI
- User publishes AI poems commercially without seeking consent
- User claims: (a) AI training is fair use/TDM, (b) output is transformative

**Legal Questions:**
- Does TDM exception cover generative AI training?
- Is AI output a "derivative work" under copyright law?
- Can contractual restriction (Palimpsest) override statutory exception?
- Is AI output sufficiently "transformative" to be non-infringing?

**Jurisdictions:**
- **Best:** Netherlands or EU (TDM exception is opt-out; Palimpsest = opt-out)
- **Challenging:** US (fair use doctrine; transformative use arguments)

**Enforcement Strategy:**
1. Cease-and-desist citing license violation
2. Copyright infringement claim (if output is derivative)
3. Contract breach claim (used for prohibited purpose)
4. Seek injunction + damages
5. Public accountability (if user is commercial entity)

**Pro Bono Support:**
- Copyright scholars (fair use vs. contract debate)
- AI ethics organisations (Partnership on AI, AI Now)
- Creator unions (Writers' Guild, Musicians' Union)

**Policy Impact:**
- Clarifies whether contract can restrict TDM/fair use
- Establishes AI-generated content as derivative work (or not)
- Informs copyright reform debates

## Risk Assessment & Mitigation

### Legal Risks

**Risk 1: License Ruled Unenforceable**
- **Scenario:** Court finds Palimpsest violates copyright exceptions (TDM, fair use) or public policy
- **Likelihood:** Moderate (untested license, novel legal questions)
- **Impact:** High (undermines entire project)
- **Mitigation:**
  - Obtain legal opinions from respected experts (IViR, law firms) pre-release
  - Draft conservatively (don't overreach beyond copyright scope)
  - Include severability clause (if one provision fails, rest survives)
  - Monitor case law, update license based on legal developments

**Risk 2: Conflict of Laws Issues**
- **Scenario:** Foreign court refuses to apply Dutch law, applies local law instead
- **Likelihood:** Moderate (depends on jurisdiction, facts)
- **Impact:** Moderate (reduces enforceability in that jurisdiction)
- **Mitigation:**
  - Research conflicts principles in key jurisdictions
  - Consider jurisdiction-specific versions if needed
  - Include forum selection clause (litigate in Dutch courts)
  - Engage local lawyers for jurisdiction-specific advice

**Risk 3: Platform Non-Compliance**
- **Scenario:** Platforms refuse to recognise Palimpsest, strip metadata, don't enforce
- **Likelihood:** Moderate-High (platforms resist custom licenses)
- **Impact:** High (limits practical enforceability)
- **Mitigation:**
  - Engage platforms early (technical integration discussions)
  - Develop machine-readable standards compatible with platforms
  - Public pressure campaigns if platforms resist
  - Regulatory complaints (DSA, DMA) for non-compliance
  - Partner with Creative Commons (platform relationships)

**Risk 4: AI Company Ignores License**
- **Scenario:** AI company trains on Palimpsest works, claims TDM/fair use, refuses to stop
- **Likelihood:** High (AI companies aggressively scrape)
- **Impact:** High (core violation, requires enforcement)
- **Mitigation:**
  - Document violations meticulously
  - Cease-and-desist letters (establish notice)
  - Public shaming campaigns
  - Strategic litigation (choose strong test case)
  - Regulatory complaints (AI Act, data protection)

---

### Reputational Risks

**Risk 5: Seen as Anti-Open, Anti-Remix**
- **Scenario:** Palimpsest perceived as restrictive, anti-commons, conflicting with Creative Commons ethos
- **Likelihood:** Moderate (some open culture advocates may object)
- **Impact:** Moderate (limits adoption among remix culture communities)
- **Mitigation:**
  - Clear messaging: "Pro-remix for humans, anti-exploitation by AI"
  - Partnership with Creative Commons (validate complementarity)
  - Highlight that Palimpsest allows derivatives with attribution (humans)
  - Position as protecting creators, not restricting creativity

**Risk 6: Fragmentation of Licensing Landscape**
- **Scenario:** Palimpsest adds complexity; creators confused about which license to use
- **Likelihood:** Moderate (licensing is already complex)
- **Impact:** Moderate (slows adoption)
- **Mitigation:**
  - Clear decision tree: "Use CC for most cases; use Palimpsest if AI protection critical"
  - Simple, accessible documentation
  - Avoid proliferation of variants (maintain small set of core licenses)
  - Partner with Creative Commons to clarify relationship

---

### Operational Risks

**Risk 7: Lack of Legal Resources for Enforcement**
- **Scenario:** Violations occur but no pro bono lawyers available, no funding for litigation
- **Likelihood:** High (enforcement is expensive)
- **Impact:** High (license becomes "toothless")
- **Mitigation:**
  - Build network of pro bono lawyers NOW (this document's purpose)
  - Crowdfunding for strategic litigation (community support)
  - Partner with organisations with litigation capacity (SFLC, EFF, PIIPA)
  - Alternative enforcement (public pressure, regulatory complaints)

**Risk 8: Governance Challenges**
- **Scenario:** Palimpsest Council cannot make decisions, lacks capacity, or loses legitimacy
- **Likelihood:** Moderate (volunteer governance is challenging)
- **Impact:** High (project stalls)
- **Mitigation:**
  - Clear governance procedures (documented in GOVERNANCE.md)
  - Recruit committed Council members (legal experts, creators, technologists)
  - Transparency (public documentation, community input)
  - Consider formalising as Dutch Stichting (foundation) for legal status

---

### Technical Risks

**Risk 9: Metadata Stripping Cannot Be Prevented Technically**
- **Scenario:** Despite best efforts, technical limitations allow easy metadata removal
- **Likelihood:** Moderate (technical cat-and-mouse game)
- **Impact:** Moderate (reduces practical protection)
- **Mitigation:**
  - Legal liability for stripping (even if technically possible)
  - Multiple metadata embedding (visible attribution + machine-readable + blockchain/hash)
  - Platform requirements (legal duty to preserve)
  - Quantum-proof traceability (future-proofing)
