## 1. Translation Framework

### 1.1 Legal Status of Language Versions

**Dutch Version (Nederlandse Versie):**
- **Legal Status:** Legally binding in the Kingdom of the Netherlands
- **Authority:** Primary authentic text for Dutch courts
- **Legal Basis:** Boek 6 van het Burgerlijk Wetboek (Book 6, Dutch Civil Code)
- **Register:** Formal legal Dutch (juridisch Nederlands)
- **Spelling:** Nederlandse spelling volgens Spelling 2015

**English Version:**
- **Legal Status:** Informative, internationally accessible version
- **Authority:** Not legally binding in Netherlands unless parties explicitly agree
- **Legal Tradition:** British Common Law terminology (for international comprehension)
- **Register:** Formal legal English
- **Spelling:** British English (colour, licence, organisation, not color, license, organization)
- **Grammar:** British conventions (quotation marks, date formats, etc.)

### 1.2 Translation Methodology

**Functional Equivalence Approach:**
- Translates legal **effect** and **intent**, not merely words
- Identifies equivalent legal concepts between Dutch civil law and British common law systems
- Explicitly acknowledges where no direct equivalent exists
- Preserves structural parallelism (clause numbering, headings, formatting)

**Harmonised EU Terminology:**
- Uses standardised EU multilingual legal terminology where applicable
- References EUR-Lex glossary for copyright and IP terms
- Consistent with EU Directives (InfoSoc Directive 2001/29/EC, DSM Directive 2019/790)

**Cultural and Ethical Sensitivity:**
- Preserves symbolic and emotional depth of Palimpsest concepts
- Maintains accessibility and plain-language orientation
- Respects diaspora narratives, trauma stories, and cultural heritage terminology

### 1.3 Precedence Rules

**Conflict Resolution:**

In the event of discrepancy between language versions:

| **Jurisdiction** | **Prevailing Version** | **Legal Basis** |
|------------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| **Netherlands** | Dutch version prevails | Primary legal text under Dutch law |
| **Scotland** | English version used; Dutch version for interpretation | Hague Convention 2005; Scots law proceedings |
| **Other jurisdictions** | English version (or local translation if available) | International accessibility |
| **Arbitration (UNCITRAL)** | Both versions considered; Dutch version authoritative for Dutch law interpretation | Arbitration rules allow reference to both |

## 4. Translation Rationale

### 4.1 Conceptual Translation Challenges

#### 4.1.1 "Verflauwing" ‚Üí "Flattening"

**Challenge:**
- Dutch *verflauwing* derives from *verflauwen* ("to fade", "to weaken", "to lose intensity")
- No single English legal term captures this cultural/aesthetic concept

**Options Considered:**
1. **Dilution** ‚Äî too commercial (trademark law connotations)
2. **Trivialisation** ‚Äî too judgemental, lacks metaphorical depth
3. **Reductionism** ‚Äî too academic, not accessible
4. **Flattening** ‚Äî metaphor for loss of dimensionality

**Chosen Solution:** **"Flattening"**
- **Rationale**: Resonates with "layered" metaphor (palimpsest as layers); accessible to creative communities; captures loss of depth without legal jargon
- **Cultural fit**: Used in digital humanities (flattening of texts), art criticism (flattening of meaning)
- **Consistency**: Used throughout Clause 0, 2, 8

#### 4.1.2 "Emotionele afstamming" ‚Üí "Emotional lineage"

**Challenge:**
- Dutch *afstamming* = "descent", "ancestry", "lineage" (biological/genealogical)
- Applying biological metaphor to creative works requires care

**Options Considered:**
1. **Emotional descent** ‚Äî awkward phrasing
2. **Emotional ancestry** ‚Äî too literal (suggests dead ancestors)
3. **Emotional lineage** ‚Äî genealogical metaphor, cultural resonance
4. **Emotional provenance** ‚Äî too art-market focused

**Chosen Solution:** **"Emotional lineage"**
- **Rationale**: "Lineage" evokes cultural heritage, oral traditions, narrative transmission
- **Cultural sensitivity**: Respectful of diaspora narratives, Indigenous knowledge systems
- **Accessibility**: Widely understood; used in cultural studies, anthropology
- **Consistency**: Parallel with "Lineage Statement" (Clause 3.2), "Synthetic Lineage Tag" (Clause 6.3)

#### 4.1.3 "Symbolische erkenning" ‚Üí "Symbolic attribution"

**Challenge:**
- Dutch *erkenning* broader than English "attribution" (includes recognition, acknowledgement, validation)
- Copyright law uses "attribution"; Palimpsest concept extends beyond legal credit

**Options Considered:**
1. **Symbolic recognition** ‚Äî loses legal grounding
2. **Symbolic credit** ‚Äî too informal
3. **Symbolic acknowledgement** ‚Äî passive, lacks agency
4. **Symbolic attribution** ‚Äî legal term + cultural modifier

**Chosen Solution:** **"Symbolic attribution"**
- **Rationale**: Combines standard copyright term ("attribution") with Palimpsest modifier ("symbolic")
- **Legal clarity**: "Attribution" understood in CC licenses, Berne Convention
- **Cultural depth**: "Symbolic" signals beyond-legal recognition (cultural context, emotional debt)
- **Consistency**: Used throughout Clause 1, 2, 6

### 4.2 Register and Formality Decisions

#### 4.2.1 Formal Legal Language vs Accessibility

**Tension:**
- Legal precision requires formal register (juridical terminology)
- Palimpsest ethos prioritises accessibility (plain language, creative communities)

**Translation Approach:**
- **Core legal clauses** (Clauses 4, 9): Use standard legal terms (e.g. "irrevocable", "jurisdiction", "termination")
- **Cultural/ethical clauses** (Clauses 0, 1, 2, 8): Balance legal terms with accessible language (e.g. "flattening" not "dilution")
- **Definitions** (Clause Definitions): Provide plain-language explanations with legal anchors

**Example:**
- **Clause 9.2** (Dutch): *Symbolische erkenning ontbreekt*
- **Literal translation**: "Symbolic recognition is lacking"
- **Legal translation**: "Attribution failure"
- **Chosen**: "Attribution failure" ‚Äî combines legal precision ("attribution") with accessibility ("failure" clearer than "absence" or "deficiency")

#### 4.2.2 British English vs American English

**Policy:**
- **Spelling**: British English (colour, licence, organisation)
- **Grammar**: British conventions (quotation marks, date formats)
- **Legal terminology**: British legal terms where applicable

**Rationale:**
- **Jurisdiction**: Enforcement venue is Scotland (British jurisdiction)
- **International accessibility**: British English more neutral internationally than American English
- **EU alignment**: Harmonised with EU English (British-style)

**Key Differences:**
| **British English** | **American English** | **Used in Palimpsest** |
|---------------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| licence (noun) | license | licence |
| organisation | organization | organisation |
| colour | color | colour (if used) |
| judgement | judgment | judgement |

### 4.3 Cultural Concepts Without Direct Equivalents

#### 4.3.1 "Kort geding" (Dutch legal concept)

**Concept:**
- Fast-track preliminary relief proceedings before *voorzieningenrechter* (preliminary relief judge)
- No exact equivalent in English/Scottish law (closest: interim injunction, summary judgment)

**Translation Strategy:**
- **Used in Palimpsest?** Not in v0.3 text, but relevant for future dispute resolution clauses
- **Recommended translation**: "Summary proceedings" or "preliminary relief proceedings"
- **Add explanatory note**: "(Dutch fast-track procedure before preliminary relief judge)"

#### 4.3.2 "Redelijkheid en billijkheid" (Reasonableness and fairness)

**Concept:**
- Overarching principle of good faith in Dutch contract law (BW 6:248)
- Civilian concept; not exact equivalent in common law ("good faith" closest)

**Translation Strategy:**
- **Translation**: "Reasonableness and fairness" (literal)
- **Legal context**: Explain as civilian good faith principle
- **Alternative**: "Good faith and fair dealing" (common law phrasing)
- **Used in Palimpsest?** Implicit in Clause 0 (Purpose), accessibility ethos

#### 4.3.3 Palimpsest Neologisms

**Challenge:**
- Palimpsest creates new concepts (e.g. "emotional lineage", "symbolic attribution")
- These must be translated consistently, even though not standard legal terms

**Approach:**
1. **Create parallel neologism** in English (e.g. *emotionele afstamming* ‚Üí "emotional lineage")
2. **Capitalise** formal terms (e.g. "Lineage Statement", "Synthetic Lineage Tag")
3. **Maintain metaphorical coherence** (lineage, layers, flattening all relate to depth/ancestry metaphors)
4. **Document in glossary** (this bilingual map + legal lexicon)

### 5.2 Cultural Sensitivity in Translation

#### 5.2.1 Diaspora Narratives

**Dutch:** *Diaspora verhalen*
**English:** Diaspora narratives

**Cultural Context:**
- Refers to stories of displaced communities (refugees, migrants, postcolonial subjects)
- Palimpsest protects these against flattening, commodification

**Translation Care:**
- "Narratives" (not "stories") ‚Äî more formal, respects gravitas
- Capitalise when referring to specific protected category
- Avoid exoticisation or othering language

#### 5.2.2 Trauma Narratives

**Dutch:** *Trauma verhalen*
**English:** Trauma narratives

**Cultural Context:**
- Works dealing with personal/collective trauma (war, abuse, genocide, slavery)
- Requires protection against trivialisation (e.g. meme-ification)

**Translation Care:**
- Clinical term "trauma" retained (not euphemisms like "difficult experiences")
- "Narratives" respects first-person testimony and storytelling traditions
- Accessibility note: Provide content warnings where appropriate

#### 5.2.3 Indigenous Knowledge

**Dutch:** *Inheemse kennis*
**English:** Indigenous knowledge

**Cultural Context:**
- Traditional knowledge of Indigenous peoples (oral traditions, ecological knowledge, cultural practices)
- Often collectively held, not individual authorship

**Translation Care:**
- Capitalise "Indigenous" (respects sovereignty and peoplehood)
- "Knowledge" (not "folklore" or "traditions") ‚Äî avoids primitivising language
- Acknowledge collective authorship models (not Western individual author)

- Respect target legal culture (British legal terminology for English version)

- Balance accessibility with legal precision

### 6.3 Review Process

#### 6.3.1 Four-Eyes Principle (Peer Review)

**Stage 1: Initial Translation**
- Professional legal translator (Dutch ‚Üí English)

- Use terminology database and style guide

**Stage 2: Legal Review**
- Dutch legal expert reviews Dutch version

| **Dutch Phrase** | **Literal Translation** | **Functional Translation** | **Why** |
|------------------|-------------------------|----------------------------|---------|

### 7.3 Translation Workflow for Palimpsest

#### 7.3.1 Pre-Translation Phase

**Step 1: Familiarisation**
1. Read existing license versions (v0.3 Dutch and English)
2. Study this bilingual map document
3. Review legal lexicon (`legal/translations/dutch-english-legal-lexicon.md`)
4. Understand Palimpsest ethos (read `CLAUDE.md`, `README.md`, governance documents)

**Step 2: Terminology Extraction**
1. Identify all defined terms (capitalised terms, technical terms)
2. Extract new terms not in existing lexicon
3. Research legal equivalents (EUR-Lex, WIPO Pearl, legal dictionaries)
4. Draft provisional translations for new terms

**Step 3: Reference Materials**
1. Set up translation memory (TM) with existing v0.3 translations
2. Load terminology database (approved term pairs)
3. Prepare style guide (British English, formal register)
4. Gather parallel texts (EU Directives, CC licenses, GPL)

#### 7.3.2 Translation Phase

**Step 1: Clause-by-Clause Translation**
1. Translate one clause at a time (maintain structure)
2. Use approved terminology from database
3. Apply functional equivalence (legal effect, not literal words)
4. Flag untranslatable concepts for explanatory notes

**Step 2: Self-Revision**
1. Read translation without source text (fluency check)
2. Compare with source text (accuracy check)
3. Verify terminology consistency (same term always translated identically)
4. Check cross-references (clause numbers, file paths)

**Step 3: Alignment Verification**
1. Create side-by-side comparison (Dutch | English)
2. Verify clause numbering matches
3. Check list formatting (bullets, numbering)
4. Ensure capitalisation consistent (defined terms)

#### 7.3.3 Post-Translation Phase

**Step 1: Peer Review**
1. Second translator reviews (legal accuracy)
2. Legal expert reviews (functional equivalence)
3. Cultural sensitivity review (diaspora communities, accessibility advocates)

**Step 2: Bilingual Map Update**
1. Add new clauses to bilingual map (clause-by-clause alignment)
2. Document translation rationale for key terms
3. Update terminology database with new terms
4. Note any non-equivalent concepts (explanatory notes)

**Step 3: Council Review and Approval**
1. Submit to Palimpsest Stewardship Council
2. Respond to queries and revisions
3. Finalise both versions simultaneously
4. Publish with version control (Git)

### 7.4 Quality Control Checklist for Translators

#### 7.4.1 Linguistic Quality

- [ ] **Spelling**: British English (colour, licence, organisation)
- [ ] **Grammar**: British conventions (quotation marks, punctuation)
- [ ] **Register**: Formal legal language (consistent throughout)
- [ ] **Terminology**: Approved terms from database
- [ ] **Consistency**: Same term always translated identically
- [ ] **Fluency**: Reads naturally in target language (not stilted)

#### 7.4.2 Legal Accuracy

- [ ] **Functional equivalence**: Legal effect identical in both versions
- [ ] **Rights and obligations**: Same in Dutch and English
- [ ] **Prohibitions**: Aligned (what's prohibited in Dutch is prohibited in English)
- [ ] **Procedures**: Identical steps (e.g. dispute resolution)
- [ ] **Defined terms**: Capitalised and used consistently

#### 7.4.3 Structural Alignment

- [ ] **Clause numbering**: Identical (1.1, 1.2, etc.)
- [ ] **Headings**: Parallel hierarchy (H2, H3 levels)
- [ ] **Lists**: Same formatting (bullets, numbering)
- [ ] **Cross-references**: Accurate (clause numbers, file paths)
- [ ] **Emphasis**: Consistent (bold, italics for same terms)

#### 7.4.4 Cultural Sensitivity

- [ ] **Respectful language**: Diaspora, trauma, Indigenous communities
- [ ] **No exoticisation**: Avoid othering language
- [ ] **Accessibility**: Plain language where possible
- [ ] **Inclusive examples**: Diverse communities represented
- [ ] **Symbolic depth**: Emotional/cultural layers preserved

#### 7.4.5 Technical Accuracy

- [ ] **File paths**: Correct and working links
- [ ] **Metadata**: JSON-LD valid (if included)
- [ ] **Code snippets**: Functional (if included)
- [ ] **Formatting**: Markdown syntax correct
- [ ] **Version numbers**: Consistent across documents
